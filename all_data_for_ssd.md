## About Samta Sainik Dal

Samta Sainik Dal (SSD) is a social volunteer organization created by Dr. B.R. Ambedkar to safeguard the rights of Dalits and other oppressed sections of Indian society through organized, disciplined mass action.[^7][^4][^8][^5]
It works as “an army of social equality”, uniting youth and community members to fight all inequality based on caste, religion, gender, class and race, and to help build a society founded on human dignity and equal rights.[^9][^6][^7]

SSD is non‑political in its core self‑definition but historically has worked alongside Ambedkar’s political organizations such as the Independent Labour Party and the Scheduled Castes Federation, providing disciplined volunteers and protection during mass actions.[^3][^5]

***

## Founder and origins

Dr. Bhimrao Ramji Ambedkar, a jurist, economist and chief architect of the Indian Constitution, founded Samta Sainik Dal as part of his broader movement to annihilate caste and secure social, political and economic equality for the oppressed.[^10][^11][^12][^1]
After returning from London in the 1920s, Ambedkar built several organizations and publications—such as Bahishkrit Hitakarini Sabha (1924), Mooknayak and Bahishkrit Bharat—to awaken Dalits, and he soon realized the need for a dedicated volunteer force to defend people and his meetings from attacks.[^8][^1][^10]

According to Marathi and Hindi sources, Ambedkar established Samta Sainik Dal around the Mahad movement, with many traditions tracing its foundation to a conference at Mahad on 24 September 1924, before the famous Mahad Satyagraha.[^4][^8]
Other historical accounts highlight March 1927, when Ambedkar announced the formation of a “Social Equality Army (Samata Sainik Dal)” during the Chavdar Lake struggle at Mahad, evolving from protective bodies like Bhim Sevak Dal and Dr Ambedkar Seva Dal.[^13][^1][^10][^5][^3]

Because of these overlapping milestones, different wings of SSD observe foundation day on dates such as 24 September or mid‑March, but all agree that the Dal emerged from Ambedkar’s need for a disciplined force to defend Dalit rights in the 1924–1927 Mahad phase.[^2][^5][^4][^13][^3]

## Historical timeline (1924–present)

Use this as a History/Timeline page or section.

- 1920–1923: Ambedkar publishes Mooknayak and completes advanced studies abroad, returning with a clear program for Dalit emancipation.[^1][^10]
- 1924: Bahishkrit Hitakarini Sabha is formed to work for the uplift of “Depressed Classes”; around this period Ambedkar conceptualizes a volunteer corps to protect meetings and mobilizations.[^10][^8][^1]
- 24 September 1924: Mahad conference is held; several sources record this date as the formation of Samta Sainik Dal to organize Dalit youth and protect villages from caste‑violence.[^4][^8]
- 1926–1927: To handle threats ahead of the Mahad Satyagraha, Bhim Sevak Dal and related volunteer groups are set up, leading to Ambedkar’s formal announcement of a “Social Equality Army (Samata Sainik Dal)” during the Chavdar Lake struggle in March 1927.[^5][^1][^3][^10]
- Late 1920s: SSD volunteers protect Ambedkar’s meetings, help maintain discipline in rallies, and act as a shield against attacks when caste Hindu groups respond violently to the growing self‑respect movement.[^3][^5][^4]
- 1930s: SSD evolves as a non‑political wing allied to Ambedkar’s Independent Labour Party; Ambedkar repeatedly calls on SSD to educate the masses and participate in electoral mobilization, including Bombay Assembly elections.[^13][^5][^3]
- 1940s: With the formation of the Scheduled Castes Federation, SSD functions as a supplementary force responsible for mobilization and security at major conventions such as the 1942 Nagpur session.[^9][^3]
- Post‑1956: After Ambedkar’s passing, SSD experiences decline in some regions but is revived by Ambedkarite activists who rebuild it as a nationwide volunteer organization connected with the Republican movement and Buddhist organizations.[^6][^5][^3]
- Late 20th century to 2000s: All India Samta Sainik Dal (AISSD) and related bodies consolidate SSD’s national presence, with headquarters at Nagpur and units across multiple states, continuing work in social equality, education and protection of Ambedkarite events.[^14][^15][^6]
- 2010s–2020s: New official and community‑run websites, social media pages and local branches (including in Delhi) highlight SSD’s work in health, education, environment, women’s empowerment and youth development through disciplined volunteerism.[^16][^17][^18][^6]
- 2024–2027: The organization moves “towards centenary” as different branches celebrate 100+ years of SSD based on their chosen foundation date, emphasizing self‑defence, discipline and building a casteless society.[^19][^2][^13]

## Ideology, vision and mission

The core principle of Samta Sainik Dal is to establish equality among the masses of Indian society by annihilating the caste system and all forms of social hierarchy.[^18][^7][^6][^9]
The official constitution states that SSD’s aim is “to strive for the removal of all inequality based on race, religion, caste, sex or class and to unite all members of the Scheduled Caste communities for a struggle to build a society based on the freedom and equality of all.”[^7][^9]

From this, the vision and mission for your website can be written as:

- Vision: A casteless, democratic, humane India where every human being enjoys equal rights, self‑respect and dignity, free from all forms of discrimination.[^6][^7][^9]
- Mission: To organize and train volunteers as “soldiers for equality” who educate, mobilize and protect oppressed communities; resist injustice through disciplined, non‑violent struggle; and work for equality in education, employment, health, environment and social life.[^16][^18][^7][^5]

Ambedkar’s own statements treat SSD as an inseparable part of his movement, insisting that without such a volunteer corps the struggle against powerful, organized opponents would not be sustainable.[^8][^13][^3]

## Values and guiding principles

You can list SSD’s values as a section:

- Equality: Rejecting caste, gender, religious and economic hierarchies in thought, speech and practice, and treating every person as fully human.[^7][^9][^6]
- Liberty and Fraternity: Building a society where oppressed communities enjoy real freedom with a sense of brotherhood and mutual respect.[^9][^13][^7]
- Discipline and Courage: Each “Sainik” (volunteer) is expected to be brave, disciplined and ready to sacrifice personal comfort to defend human rights, as reflected in the Sainik’s Oath.[^8][^7][^9]
- Non‑violence with self‑respect: Ambedkar emphasized peaceful methods while rejecting meekness; SSD stands for self‑defence and firm resistance against oppression without hatred or blind violence.[^19][^5][^3]
- Service to humanity: SSD is described as a purely social organization committed to workers, labourers, Dalits, exploited and poor people, tasked with alleviating their pain and raising their standard of life.[^2][^18][^6]

## Organizational structure

The SSD constitution describes a detailed, almost military‑style organizational hierarchy.[^20][^21][^9]
At the national level, the Supreme Commander‑in‑Chief is linked to the leadership of The Buddhist Society of India, while the National President of SSD functions as Commander‑in‑Chief of the force; a National General Secretary of the Buddhist Society acts as Joint Commander‑in‑Chief.[^15][^20]

From there, the structure proceeds as follows:

- National level: Supreme Commander‑in‑Chief, Commander‑in‑Chief, Joint Commander‑in‑Chief, Staff Officer and Assistant Staff Officer supervise the entire force.[^21][^20]
- Zonal and state (Regiment) level: Zones and Regiments (states) are commanded by Major‑General or equivalent officers (GOC and Assistant GOC) responsible for state‑wide organization.[^21]
- Division, Battalion, Company and Platoon: These layers correspond roughly to province/district, city/taluka, block/sector and mohalla/village levels, each with its own officers and committee.[^21][^9]
- Branches and units: Each town or village has a General Body of SSD members, which elects a local committee with a President, Secretaries and delegates; in any branch, the President and Secretary act as GOC and Deputy GOC.[^20][^9][^21]

Your Delhi‑unit website can explain that it functions as a branch/sector under this national framework, with its own President, Secretary and officers while remaining affiliated to All India Samta Sainik Dal and the broader SSD network.[^15][^18][^6]

## Frequently asked questions (FAQ)

You can adapt this FAQ section directly for your website.

**Q1. When was Samta Sainik Dal founded?**
Historical sources differ: some record 24 September 1924 at a Mahad conference as the founding date, while others emphasize Ambedkar’s announcement of a “Social Equality Army” during the Mahad struggle in March 1927; many branches celebrate foundation day on one of these dates.[^4][^13][^3][^5][^8]

**Q2. Who founded Samta Sainik Dal and why?**
Dr. B.R. Ambedkar founded SSD to create a disciplined volunteer force that could protect Dalits and other oppressed people, defend his meetings from attacks and help organize a mass struggle against caste‑based inequality.[^10][^1][^4][^3][^5]

**Q3. Is SSD a political party?**
SSD is defined in its constitution as a social organization devoted to removing inequality and uniting Scheduled Caste communities, though historically it has worked closely with Ambedkar’s political fronts such as the Independent Labour Party and Scheduled Castes Federation.[^13][^3][^5][^7][^9]

**Q4. What is the relationship between SSD Delhi and All India Samta Sainik Dal?**
SSD Delhi is a branch/unit functioning within the all‑India organizational structure, guided by national leadership based in Nagpur/Mumbai and North‑zone coordinators, while focusing specifically on the needs and struggles of Delhi’s oppressed communities.[^23][^6][^15][^18][^2]

**Q5. What kind of training do Sainiks receive?**
SSD emphasizes ideological education in Ambedkar’s thought, training in discipline and public conduct, and preparation for non‑violent self‑defence, first‑aid and crowd management to protect meetings, rallies and vulnerable communities.[^19][^3][^5][^7][^9]

**Q6. How does SSD fund its activities?**
Public sources emphasize SSD’s character as a voluntary social organization; branches typically depend on member contributions, community donations and support from sympathizers, as seen in appeals for donations and branch formation on AISSD platforms.[^6][^18]

**Q7. Is SSD active outside Maharashtra and Delhi?**
Yes. Online records show SSD or AISSD activities and branch interest across multiple states including Maharashtra, Rajasthan, Uttar Pradesh, Punjab, Gujarat and others, making it a truly all‑India Ambedkarite volunteer network.[^24][^14][^15][^6][^18]
