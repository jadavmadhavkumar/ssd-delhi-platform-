## About Samta Sainik Dal

Samta Sainik Dal (SSD) is a social volunteer organization created by
Dr. B.R. Ambedkar to safeguard the rights of Dalits and other oppressed
sections of Indian society through organized, disciplined mass
action.[^11][^12][^13][^14] It works as "an army of social equality",
uniting youth and community members to fight all inequality based on
caste, religion, gender, class and race, and to help build a society
founded on human dignity and equal rights.[^15][^16][^17]

SSD is non‑political in its core self‑definition but historically has
worked alongside Ambedkar's political organizations such as the
Independent Labour Party and the Scheduled Castes Federation, providing
disciplined volunteers and protection during mass actions.[^18][^19]

## Founder and origins

Dr. Bhimrao Ramji Ambedkar, a jurist, economist and chief architect of
the Indian Constitution, founded Samta Sainik Dal as part of his broader
movement to annihilate caste and secure social, political and economic
equality for the oppressed.[^20][^21][^22][^23] After returning from
London in the 1920s, Ambedkar built several organizations and
publications---such as Bahishkrit Hitakarini Sabha (1924), Mooknayak and
Bahishkrit Bharat---to awaken Dalits, and he soon realized the need for
a dedicated volunteer force to defend people and his meetings from
attacks.[^24][^25][^26]

According to Marathi and Hindi sources, Ambedkar established Samta
Sainik Dal around the Mahad movement, with many traditions tracing its
foundation to a conference at Mahad on 24 September 1924, before the
famous Mahad Satyagraha.[^27][^28] Other historical accounts highlight
March 1927, when Ambedkar announced the formation of a "Social Equality
Army (Samata Sainik Dal)" during the Chavdar Lake struggle at Mahad,
evolving from protective bodies like Bhim Sevak Dal and Dr Ambedkar Seva
Dal.[^29][^30][^31][^32][^33]

Because of these overlapping milestones, different wings of SSD observe
foundation day on dates such as 24 September or mid‑March, but all agree
that the Dal emerged from Ambedkar's need for a disciplined force to
defend Dalit rights in the 1924--1927 Mahad
phase.[^34][^35][^36][^37][^38]

## Historical timeline (1924--present)

Use this as a History/Timeline page or section.

-   1920--1923: Ambedkar publishes Mooknayak and completes advanced
    studies abroad, returning with a clear program for Dalit
    emancipation.[^39][^40]
-   1924: Bahishkrit Hitakarini Sabha is formed to work for the uplift
    of "Depressed Classes"; around this period Ambedkar conceptualizes a
    volunteer corps to protect meetings and
    mobilizations.[^41][^42][^43]
-   24 September 1924: Mahad conference is held; several sources record
    this date as the formation of Samta Sainik Dal to organize Dalit
    youth and protect villages from caste‑violence.[^44][^45]
-   1926--1927: To handle threats ahead of the Mahad Satyagraha, Bhim
    Sevak Dal and related volunteer groups are set up, leading to
    Ambedkar's formal announcement of a "Social Equality Army (Samata
    Sainik Dal)" during the Chavdar Lake struggle in March
    1927.[^46][^47][^48][^49]
-   Late 1920s: SSD volunteers protect Ambedkar's meetings, help
    maintain discipline in rallies, and act as a shield against attacks
    when caste Hindu groups respond violently to the growing
    self‑respect movement.[^50][^51][^52]
-   1930s: SSD evolves as a non‑political wing allied to Ambedkar's
    Independent Labour Party; Ambedkar repeatedly calls on SSD to
    educate the masses and participate in electoral mobilization,
    including Bombay Assembly elections.[^53][^54][^55]
-   1940s: With the formation of the Scheduled Castes Federation, SSD
    functions as a supplementary force responsible for mobilization and
    security at major conventions such as the 1942 Nagpur
    session.[^56][^57]
-   Post‑1956: After Ambedkar's passing, SSD experiences decline in some
    regions but is revived by Ambedkarite activists who rebuild it as a
    nationwide volunteer organization connected with the Republican
    movement and Buddhist organizations.[^58][^59][^60]
-   Late 20th century to 2000s: All India Samta Sainik Dal (AISSD) and
    related bodies consolidate SSD's national presence, with
    headquarters at Nagpur and units across multiple states, continuing
    work in social equality, education and protection of Ambedkarite
    events.[^61][^62][^63]
-   2010s--2020s: New official and community‑run websites, social media
    pages and local branches (including in Delhi) highlight SSD's work
    in health, education, environment, women's empowerment and youth
    development through disciplined volunteerism.[^64][^65][^66][^67]
-   2024--2027: The organization moves "towards centenary" as different
    branches celebrate 100+ years of SSD based on their chosen
    foundation date, emphasizing self‑defence, discipline and building a
    casteless society.[^68][^69][^70]

## Ideology, vision and mission

The core principle of Samta Sainik Dal is to establish equality among
the masses of Indian society by annihilating the caste system and all
forms of social hierarchy.[^71][^72][^73][^74] The official constitution
states that SSD's aim is "to strive for the removal of all inequality
based on race, religion, caste, sex or class and to unite all members of
the Scheduled Caste communities for a struggle to build a society based
on the freedom and equality of all."[^75][^76]

From this, the vision and mission for your website can be written as:

-   Vision: A casteless, democratic, humane India where every human
    being enjoys equal rights, self‑respect and dignity, free from all
    forms of discrimination.[^77][^78][^79]
-   Mission: To organize and train volunteers as "soldiers for equality"
    who educate, mobilize and protect oppressed communities; resist
    injustice through disciplined, non‑violent struggle; and work for
    equality in education, employment, health, environment and social
    life.[^80][^81][^82][^83]

Ambedkar's own statements treat SSD as an inseparable part of his
movement, insisting that without such a volunteer corps the struggle
against powerful, organized opponents would not be
sustainable.[^84][^85][^86]

## Values and guiding principles

You can list SSD's values as a section:

-   Equality: Rejecting caste, gender, religious and economic
    hierarchies in thought, speech and practice, and treating every
    person as fully human.[^87][^88][^89]
-   Liberty and Fraternity: Building a society where oppressed
    communities enjoy real freedom with a sense of brotherhood and
    mutual respect.[^90][^91][^92]
-   Discipline and Courage: Each "Sainik" (volunteer) is expected to be
    brave, disciplined and ready to sacrifice personal comfort to defend
    human rights, as reflected in the Sainik's Oath.[^93][^94][^95]
-   Non‑violence with self‑respect: Ambedkar emphasized peaceful methods
    while rejecting meekness; SSD stands for self‑defence and firm
    resistance against oppression without hatred or blind
    violence.[^96][^97][^98]
-   Service to humanity: SSD is described as a purely social
    organization committed to workers, labourers, Dalits, exploited and
    poor people, tasked with alleviating their pain and raising their
    standard of life.[^99][^100][^101]

## Organizational structure

The SSD constitution describes a detailed, almost military‑style
organizational hierarchy.[^102][^103][^104] At the national level, the
Supreme Commander‑in‑Chief is linked to the leadership of The Buddhist
Society of India, while the National President of SSD functions as
Commander‑in‑Chief of the force; a National General Secretary of the
Buddhist Society acts as Joint Commander‑in‑Chief.[^105][^106]

From there, the structure proceeds as follows:

-   National level: Supreme Commander‑in‑Chief, Commander‑in‑Chief,
    Joint Commander‑in‑Chief, Staff Officer and Assistant Staff Officer
    supervise the entire force.[^107][^108]
-   Zonal and state (Regiment) level: Zones and Regiments (states) are
    commanded by Major‑General or equivalent officers (GOC and Assistant
    GOC) responsible for state‑wide organization.[^109]
-   Division, Battalion, Company and Platoon: These layers correspond
    roughly to province/district, city/taluka, block/sector and
    mohalla/village levels, each with its own officers and
    committee.[^110][^111]
-   Branches and units: Each town or village has a General Body of SSD
    members, which elects a local committee with a President,
    Secretaries and delegates; in any branch, the President and
    Secretary act as GOC and Deputy GOC.[^112][^113][^114]

Your Delhi‑unit website can explain that it functions as a branch/sector
under this national framework, with its own President, Secretary and
officers while remaining affiliated to All India Samta Sainik Dal and
the broader SSD network.[^115][^116][^117]

"Sainik" takes when joining the Dal.[^118][^119] In essence, the Sainik
swears to be an honourable, brave, disciplined and resolute fighter for
the emancipation of their community, always ready to defend just and
human rights, and willing to face punishment from the Dal if they betray
this cause.[^120][^121]

-   A commitment to stand against oppression, exploitation and slavery

-   An agreement to follow the discipline, rules and collective
    decisions of SSD with honesty and courage.[^128][^129][^130]

North India and national SSD frameworks, composed of Ambedkarite

stronger organization and communication at the all‑India level.[^145]

## Frequently asked questions (FAQ)

You can adapt this FAQ section directly for your website.

**Q1. When was Samta Sainik Dal founded?** Historical sources differ:
some record 24 September 1924 at a Mahad conference as the founding
date, while others emphasize Ambedkar's announcement of a "Social
Equality Army" during the Mahad struggle in March 1927; many branches
celebrate foundation day on one of these
dates.[^239][^240][^241][^242][^243]

**Q2. Who founded Samta Sainik Dal and why?** Dr. B.R. Ambedkar founded
SSD to create a disciplined volunteer force that could protect Dalits
and other oppressed people, defend his meetings from attacks and help
organize a mass struggle against caste‑based
inequality.[^244][^245][^246][^247][^248]

**Q3. Is SSD a political party?** SSD is defined in its constitution as
a social organization devoted to removing inequality and uniting
Scheduled Caste communities, though historically it has worked closely
with Ambedkar's political fronts such as the Independent Labour Party
and Scheduled Castes Federation.[^249][^250][^251][^252][^253]

**Q4. What is the relationship between SSD Delhi and All India Samta
Sainik Dal?** SSD Delhi is a branch/unit functioning within the
all‑India organizational structure, guided by national leadership based
in Nagpur/Mumbai and North‑zone coordinators, while focusing
specifically on the needs and struggles of Delhi's oppressed
communities.[^254][^255][^256][^257][^258]

**Q5. What kind of training do Sainiks receive?** SSD emphasizes
ideological education in Ambedkar's thought, training in discipline and
public conduct, and preparation for non‑violent self‑defence, first‑aid
and crowd management to protect meetings, rallies and vulnerable
communities.[^259][^260][^261][^262][^263]

**Q6. How does SSD fund its activities?** Public sources emphasize SSD's
character as a voluntary social organization; branches typically depend
on member contributions, community donations and support from
sympathizers, as seen in appeals for donations and branch formation on
AISSD platforms.[^264][^265]

**Q7. Is SSD active outside Maharashtra and Delhi?** Yes. Online records
show SSD or AISSD activities and branch interest across multiple states
including Maharashtra, Rajasthan, Uttar Pradesh, Punjab, Gujarat and
others, making it a truly all‑India Ambedkarite volunteer
network.[^266][^267][^268][^269][^270]
